Are We Recognizing the Jaguar or Its Background?
A Diagnostic Framework for Jaguar Re-Identification

Antonio Rueda-Toicen1,2, Abigail Allen Martin3,4, Daniil Morozov5,8, Matin Mahmood8, Alexandra Schild1, Shahabeddin Dayani6, Davide Panza7, Gerard de Melo1

Abstract

Jaguar re-identification (re-ID) from citizen-science imagery can look strong on standard retrieval metrics while still relying on the wrong evidence, such as background context or silhouette shape, instead of the coat pattern that defines identity. We introduce a diagnostic framework for wildlife re-ID with two axes: a leakage-controlled context ratio, background/foreground, computed from inpainted background-only versus foreground-only images, and a laterality diagnostic based on cross-flank retrieval and mirror self-similarity. To make these diagnostics measurable, we curate a Pantanal jaguar benchmark with per-pixel segmentation masks and an identity-balanced evaluation protocol. We then use representative mitigation families, ArcFace fine-tuning, anti-symmetry regularization, and Lorentz hyperbolic embeddings, as case studies under the same evaluation lens. The goal is not only to ask which model ranks best, but also what visual evidence it uses to do so.

Key Contributions

C1 — Diagnostic Framework

Two diagnostic axes for wildlife re-ID with segmentation masks: BG/FG, a leakage-controlled measure of context reliance computed from inpainted backgrounds, and Mirror Similarity, a laterality diagnostic for bilaterally asymmetric species. We show these axes are complementary: excelling on one provides no guarantee about the other.

C2 — Jaguar Benchmark with Segmentation Masks

A citizen-science Pantanal jaguar dataset with 1,895 RGBA images, per-pixel segmentation masks, and 31 identified individuals, with a reproducible evaluation protocol via two public Kaggle challenges using identity-balanced mAP as the official metric. The dataset is publicly available on Hugging Face.

C3 — Empirical Audit of Mitigation Families

We evaluate representative mitigation families as case studies under the two-axis diagnostic framework: ArcFace fine-tuning, anti-symmetry regularization, and Lorentz hyperbolic embeddings, and report their accuracy-robustness trade-offs.

Diagnostic Framework

Six diagnostic image variants derived from a single citizen-science photograph using its SAM-3 alpha mask. (A1) Original full RGB input. (A2) Binary silhouette (alpha channel). (B1) Background with silhouette: foreground replaced by a black silhouette, retaining background context and shape cues. (B2) Inpainted: foreground removed by FLUX.1-Fill generative inpainting, eliminating the silhouette-shaped hole for leakage-controlled BG/FG evaluation. (C1) Foreground cutout (background removed), isolating coat pattern. (C2) Horizontally flipped mirror cutout, used for laterality analysis.

Axis 1: Background Context Ratio (BG/FG)

Measures how much a model relies on background context versus coat pattern. Computed as mAP(background_inpainted) / mAP(foreground_only) using identity-balanced macro mAP. Lower values indicate more pattern-focused models.

BG/FG < 0.95: Low risk  |  0.95–1.10: Medium  |  > 1.10: High risk

Axis 2: Mirror Similarity

Measures laterality awareness for species with asymmetric bilateral patterns. Jaguar rosettes differ between left and right flanks, so a horizontally flipped image depicts an individual that does not exist. High mirror similarity indicates the model collapses left–right flank differences.

< 0.85: Aware  |  0.85–0.96: Moderate  |  > 0.96: Strong shortcut

Key Findings

  • 3 of 14 frozen models have BG/FG ≥ 1.0, meaning they extract more identity signal from background context than from coat pattern.
  • Wildlife-specific pre-training is the primary driver of robustness: MiewID-MSv2 and the generic EfficientNetV2-M share the same backbone, yet their BG/FG ratios differ sharply (0.52 vs. 1.03).
  • High accuracy does not guarantee low shortcut reliance. DINOv3-ViT CLS reaches 0.298 macro mAP with BG/FG 0.77.
  • The two diagnostic axes are complementary: EVA-02 has low BG/FG (0.66) but near-perfect mirror symmetry (0.997); I-JEPA has high BG/FG (1.17) yet lower mirror similarity (0.968).
  • Flip augmentation does not improve cross-flank retrieval and reduces within-flank accuracy by 12%.
  • Lorentz hyperbolic embeddings with a solidity-conditioned radius prior yield the strongest retrieval model (Lorentz O1/MSv3, macro mAP 0.557), while the leaderboard and the laterality diagnostic reward different kinds of success.

Cross-Flank Retrieval

Jaguar coat patterns are bilaterally asymmetric: the rosette layout on the left flank differs from the right. A horizontal flip of a left-flank image is not a real observation of that animal's right flank. We test whether flip augmentation helps cross-flank matching by training matched ArcFace+MiewID-MSv2 models with and without horizontal flips.

Cross-flank macro mAP is nearly identical between models (0.477 vs. 0.490), while within-flank mAP drops from 0.716 to 0.632 with flips. The higher cross/within ratio for the flip model is driven entirely by its lower within-flank performance, not by improved cross-flank matching.
